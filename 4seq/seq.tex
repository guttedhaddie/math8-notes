\section{Sequences as Functions}

We've seen several types of function in this course and used them to model various situations. In practice, one is often faced with the opposite problem: given experimental data, what type of function should you try?

\subsection{Polynomial Sequences: First, Second, and Higher Differences}

To begin to answer this, it is helpful to think of a very basic type of function: a \emph{sequence.} Loosely speaking, a sequence a function whose domain is a set like the natural numbers, for example\par
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.65\linewidth}\vspace{-12pt}
	\[
		f:\N\to\R:n\mapsto 3n^2-2
	\]
	defines the sequence
	\[
		\big(f(1),f(2),f(3),\ldots\big)
		=\big(1,10,25,46,73,\ldots\big)
	\]
	Sequences are an intuitive way to expose even elementary school students to the idea of a function without requiring any formal acknowledgement of a formula/rule or even a domain: the \emph{first} term is 1, the \emph{second} is 10, the \emph{third} is 25, etc.
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.34\linewidth}\vspace{-4pt}
	\hfill\includegraphics[alt={quadratic data plot}]{seqquadex}
\end{minipage}\smallbreak
Such a function might arise from an experiment; indeed it is particularly common to take measurements at fixed time intervals. Suppose this has been done, and that what you therefore have is the data set
\[
	\begin{array}{c|ccccc}
		x&1&2&3&4&5\\\hline
		y&1&10&25&46&73
	\end{array}
\]
Could you recover the function $y=f(x)=3x^2-2$ directly from this data? Plotting the data (as above) is a good place to start, though it is hard to decide directly from the plot whether we should be hunting for a quadratic function, or some other power function/polynomial, or even an exponential. Of course, the physical source of real-world data might also provide clues.
\medbreak

A more mathematical approach involves considering how data values \emph{change}:
\begin{quote}
	\includegraphics[alt={first differences and second differences for the sequence y}]{xy1}
\end{quote}
%\[
% 	\xymatrix @R0pt @C15pt{
% 		x & 1 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+1}} & 2 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+1}} & 3 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+1}} & 4 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+1}} & 5\\
% 		y & 1 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{+9}}="a" & 10 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{+15}}="b" & 25 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{+21}}="c" & 46 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{+27}}="d" & 73
% 		\ar @/_/_{\textcolor{red}{+6}} "a";"b" \ar @/_/_{\textcolor{red}{+6}} "b";"c" \ar @/_/_{\textcolor{red}{+6}} "c";"d"
% 	}
%\]
The \textcolor{Blue}{first-differences} in the $x$-values are constant whereas those for the $y$-values are increasing
\[
	\bigl(y_{n+1}-y_n\bigr)=\bigl(9,15,21,27,\ldots\bigr)
\]
You likely already notice the pattern: the sequence of first-differences is increasing \emph{linearly} as the \emph{arithmetic sequence}
\[
	y_{n+1}-y_n=3+6n
\]
To make this even clearer, note that the sequence of \textcolor{mRed}{second-differences} in the $y$-values is \emph{constant} ($+6$). These facts are huge clues that we expect a quadratic function.
\medbreak


But why? Well we can certainly check the following directly: 
\begin{description}
  \item[Linear Model] If $f(n)=an+b$, then the sequence of first-differences is constant
  \[
  	f(n+1)-f(n)=a
  \]
  \item[Quadratic Model] If $f(n)=an^2+bn+c$, then the sequence of first-differences is linear and the second-differences are constant:
  \[
  	g(n):=f(n+1)-f(n)=2an+a+b,\qquad g(n+1)-g(n)=2a \tag{$\ast$}
  \]
\end{description}
  
The relationship between these results and the \emph{derivative(s)} of the original function $f(x)$ should feel intuitive: what happens if you differentiate a quadratic twice? The sequential approach in this chapter is a form of \emph{discrete calculus}: using a pattern of \emph{differences} to predict the original function is similar to how we use knowledge of a derivative $f'(x)$ to find $f(x)$.

\begin{example}{}{easyquadmodel}
	You are given the following data set\par
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.65\linewidth}\vspace{-10pt}
		\[
			\begin{array}{c|cccccc}
				x&0&2&4&6&8&10\\\hline
				y&2&16&22&20&10&-8
			\end{array}
		\]
		The $x$-values have constant first-differences while the $y$-values have constant second-differences
		\begin{itemize}
		  \item[]First-differences:\lstsp 14, 6, $-2$, $-10$, $-18$
		  \item[]Second-differences:\lstsp $-8,-8,-8,-8$
		\end{itemize}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.34\linewidth}\vspace{-10pt}
	\hfill\includegraphics[alt={quadratic data}]{seqquadex2}
\end{minipage}
\bigbreak

We therefore \emph{suspect} a quadratic model $y=f(x)=ax^2+bx+c$. But how to find it?\smallbreak
We \emph{could} try using the above formulæ ($\ast$), but this is ugly, not least because the constant $x$-differences are not 1. It is easier just to substitute in the first three values:
\[
	\begin{cases}
		2=f(0)=c\\
		16=f(2)=4a+2b+c\\
		22=f(4)=16a+4b+c
	\end{cases}
	\implies
	\begin{cases}
		c=2\\
		4a+2b=14\\
		8a+2b=10
	\end{cases}
	\implies 4a=-4
\]
whence $a=-1$, $b=9$ and $c=2$. A quadratic model is therefore
\[
	y=f(x)=-x^2+9x+2
\]
It is easily verified that the remaining data values satisfy this relationship. Indeed, we could have used substituted any three data pairs from the table and found the same model.

\subsubsection*{Limitations of the Sequential Approach}

Consider the question we're answering in Example \ref{ex:easyquadmodel}, ``Find a model satisfying given data.'' There are at least two issues with our method:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Constant second-differences don't guarantee that \emph{only} a quadratic model is suitable. For example,
  \[
  	y=-n^2+9n+2 +n(n-2)(n-4)(n-6)(n-8)(n-10)
  \]
  is a very complicated model satisfying the same given data set! Our old model would have predicted $y(1)=10$, but the new model gives $y(1)=-935$ (!). Since the usefulness of a model often lies in its ability to \emph{predict} as yet unseen results, this is potentially a big problem. Of course simplicity is also a desired property of a model, and our original quadratic certainly provides that.

  \item Experimental data is very unlikely to fit such precise patterns: the real-world is noisy with all sorts of error, not least that of the observer. However, if the differences are \emph{close} to satisfying such patterns, then you should feel confident that a linear/quadratic model is a good choice.
\end{enumerate}



\begin{example}{}{almostquadseq}
	Given the data set\par
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.65\linewidth}\vspace{-10pt}
		\[
			\begin{array}{c|cccccc}
				x&0&2&4&6&8&10\\\hline
				y&3&23&41&59&77&93
			\end{array}
		\]
		with sequences of first- and second-differences
		\begin{itemize}
	  	\item[]First-differences: 20, 18, 18, 18, 16
	  	\item[]Second-differences: $-2$, 0, 0, $-2$ 
		\end{itemize}
	\end{minipage}
	\hfill
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.34\linewidth}\vspace{-10pt}
		\hfill\includegraphics[alt={almost linear data}]{seqquadex3}
	\end{minipage}\smallbreak
	do you think a linear or quadratic model would be superior?\smallbreak
	If you wanted a linear model, you'd likely be inclined to try $f(x)=9x+b$ for some constant $b$. Here are two options:
	\begin{enumerate}
	  \item $f(x)=9x+5$ fits the middle four data values perfectly, but as a predictor is too large at the endpoints: $f(0)=5>3$ and $f(10)=95>93$.
	  \item $f(x)=9x+5-\frac 23$ doesn't pass through any of the data values but seems to reduce the net error to zero:

		\]

	Neither model is perfect, but then this is what you expect with real-world data!\smallbreak

\subsection{Exponential, Logarithmic \& Power Sequences}

Instaed of differences, you might also have to consider \emph{ratios} between successive terms.

\begin{example}{}{}
	Consider the data below. At first glance, you might believe that the data to be approximately quadratic, but how can we tell? Well, \emph{if} the data were quadratic, \emph{then} we should get constant second-differences. However, applying the constant-difference method doesn't seem to yield anything helpful:\vspace{-10pt}
	\begin{center}
		\includegraphics[alt={successive y values are in constant ratio}]{xy2}
		\qquad\qquad
		\includegraphics[alt={the data points plotted}]{seqquadex4}
	\end{center}
% 		\[
% 			\xymatrix @R0pt @C15pt{
% 				x & 1 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+2}} & 3 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+2}} &  5 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+2}} & 7 \\
% 				y & 15 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{+120}}="a" & 135 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{+1080}}="b" & 1215 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{+9720}}="c" & 10935 
% 				\ar @/_/_{\textcolor{red}{+960}} "a";"b" \ar @/_/_{\textcolor{red}{+8640}} "b";"c" 
% 			}
% 		\]
	Regardless, since we only have two \textcolor{mRed}{second-differences}, any conclusion relying on this would be very weak.	If instead we think about successive \textcolor{mGreen}{ratios} of $y$-values, then a different pattern emerges:
% 	\[
% 		\xymatrix @R0pt @C15pt{
% 			x & 1 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+2}} & 3 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+2}} &  5 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+2}} & 7 \\
% 			y & 15 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{\times 9}} & 135 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{\times 9}} & 1215 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{\times 9}} & 10935 
% 		}
% 	\]
	the output scales by 9 when 2 is added to the input, $f(x+2)=9f(x)$. \emph{Exponential} functions convert addition to multiplication,\footnotemark{} so we try such a function. If $y=f(x)=ba^x$ for some constants $a,b$, then 
	\[
		9f(x)=f(x+2)=ba^{x+2}=ba^2b^x=a^2f(x) \implies a^2=9
	\]
	A suitable model is therefore $y=5\cdot 3^x$.
\end{example}

\footnotetext{%
	This is exactly what the exponential law says: $a^{x+y}=a^x\cdot a^y$.	
}



We can see the pattern in the example more generally.

\begin{description}
	\item[Exponential Model] If $f(x)=ba^x$, then adding a constant to $x$ results in
		\[
			f(x+k)=ba^{x+k}=a^kf(x)
		\]
		If $x$-values have constant differences ($\Delta x=k$), then $y$-values will be related by a constant \emph{ratio} ($r_y= a^k$). You might remember this as `addition--product' or `arithmetic--geometric.'
\end{description}

Such a simple pattern is often disguised:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Complete data might not be given, so you might have to skip some data values to see a pattern. For example, if our original data was
  \[
  	\begin{array}[t]{c|ccccc}
			x&1&3&4&5&7\\\hline
			y&15&135&405&1215&10935
		\end{array}
	\]
	then the $x$-values are not in a strictly arithmetic sequence. Ignoring the third pair produces the exponential model above, which is then seen to satisfy $f(4)=5\cdot 3^4=405$. 
	\item As in Example \ref{ex:almostquadseq}, experimental data will likely only \emph{approximately} exhibit such patterns.
\end{itemize}



\begin{example}{}{poprabbits}
	A population $P$ of rabbits is measured every two months, producing the data set
	\[
		\begin{array}{c|cccccc}
			t&0&2&4&6&8&10\\\hline
			P&5&7&10&14&19&28
		\end{array}
	\]
	This data seems very close to being quadratic. Consider the first and second sequences of $P$-differences
	\[
		\Delta P=\big(2,3,4,5,9\big),\qquad \Delta\Delta P=\big(1,1,1,4\big)
	\]
	The last difference doesn't fit the pattern. Should we look for an exponential model instead? Indeed we \emph{expect} such a model precisely because the data is measuring population growth! We therefore consider the ratios of $P$-values:
	\[
		(r_y)=\big(1.4,1.43,1.4,1.36,1.47\big)
	\]
% 	\[
% 		\xymatrix @R0pt @C15pt{
% 			t & 0 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+2}} & 2 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+2}} & 4 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+2}} & 6 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+2}} & 8 \ar@/^/[r]^{\textcolor{blue}{+2}} & 10\\
% 			P & 5 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{\times 1.4}} & 7 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{\times 1.43}} & 10 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{\times 1.4}} & 14 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{\times 1.36}} & 19 \ar@/_/[r]_{\textcolor{blue}{\times 1.47}} & 28
% 		}
% 	\]
	The ratios are \emph{very close} to being constant $r_y\approx 1.4=\frac{28}5$, strongly suggesting an exponential model! To exactly match the first and last data values, we could take the model
	\[
		P(t)\approx 5\left(\frac{28}5\right)^{\frac t{10}}
		\qquad\quad
		\begin{array}{c|cccccc}
			t&0&2&4&6&8&10\\\hline
			P&5&7.06&9.96&14.06&19.84&28
		\end{array}
	\]
	where predictions are given to 2 decimal places. Only $P(8)$ doesn't match the original data set when rounding to the nearest integer.
\end{example}


\subsubsection*{Logarithmic and Power Models}


We've seen that addition-addition corresponds to a linear model and that addition-multiplication to an exponential. There are two other natural combinations.

\begin{description}
	\item[Logarithms] These operate exactly as exponential models but in reverse. If $f(n)=\log_a x+b$, then
		\[
			f(kx)=\log_a(kx)+b=\log_ak+\log_ax+b=\log_ak+f(x)
		\]
		Otherwise said,	\emph{multiplying} the input by a constant $r_x=k$ \emph{adds} a constant $\Delta y=\log_ak$ to the output.	This could be summarized as `product--addition.' Since it is typically easier to work with exponential models, the suggested approach to such cases is simply to switch the roles of $x$ and $y$: find an exponential model $x=pq^x$.
	\item[Power Functions] If $f(x)=ax^m$, then 
		\[
			f(kx)=a(kx)^m=ak^mx^m=k^mf(x)
		\]
		That is, \emph{multiplying} the input by a constant $r_x=k$ also \emph{multiplies} the output $r_y=k^m$.
		We have a `product--product' relationship between successive terms.
\end{description}

\subsubsection*{Predicting Values Given Limited Data}

Suppose you are given two data points. Can you predict the output for another given input? The answer depends on what type of model you choose. Here is a typical problem.


\begin{example}{}{predictex}
	Suppose a function $y=g(x)$ satisfies $g(2)=3$ and $g(4)=9$. What do you think should be the value of $g(8)$?\smallbreak
	Everything depends on the type of model you try\ldots\par
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.6\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
		\begin{enumerate}
		  \item For a linear (addition-addition) model we know that $\Delta x=4-2=2$ corresponds to $\Delta y=9-3=6$. It follows that
		  \begin{align*}
		  	\mathcolor{Blue}{g(8)} &=g(4+2\Delta x)=g(4)+2\Delta y =9+12\\
		  	&=\mathcolor{Blue}{21}
		  \end{align*}
		  \item For an exponential (addition-product) model, the constant $x$-differences $\Delta x=2$ correspond to a constant $y$-\emph{ratio} $r_y=\frac 93=3$. We therefore have
		  \begin{align*}
		  	\mathcolor{mMagenta}{g(8)} &=r_yg(6)=r_y^2g(4) =9\cdot 9\\
		  	&= \mathcolor{mMagenta}{81}
		  \end{align*}
		  \item For a power (product-product) model, $r_x=\frac 42=2$ corresponds to $r_y=3$, whence
		  \begin{align*}
		  	\mathcolor{mGreen}{g(8)} &=g(4\cdot 2)=g(4r_x)=r_yg(4) =3\cdot 9\\
		  	&=\mathcolor{mGreen}{27}
		  \end{align*}
		\end{enumerate}
	\end{minipage}
	\hfill
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.39\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
		\hfill\def\arraystretch{1.4}
		\begin{tabular}{@{}l@{}}
			\includegraphics[alt={predicting the output using different models}]{seqquadex5}\\
			\qquad\qquad 1.\lstsp $\textcolor{Blue}{g(x)=3x-3}$\\
			\qquad\qquad 2.\lstsp $\textcolor{mMagenta}{g(x)=3^{x/2}}$\\
			\qquad\qquad 3.\lstsp $\textcolor{mGreen}{g(x)=x^{\log_23}}$
		\end{tabular}
	\end{minipage}
	\par
	Notice how we didn't need to calculate the models explicitly, though they are stated below the graph for convenience. We could also use a logarithmic model to predict $g(8)$. The details of all this are left as an exercise.	
\end{example}

\subsection{Newton's Method}

Sequences are often used as a way to \emph{approximate} something useful. In this section we discuss a general approach for approximating solutions to equations.


\begin{example}{}{}
	Suppose $x_n>\sqrt 2$. Then $\frac 2{x_n}<\frac 2{\sqrt 2}=\sqrt 2$. It seems reasonable that the average
	\[
		x_{n+1}:=\frac 12\left(x_n+\frac 2{x_n}\right) \tag{$\ast$}
	\]
	should be closer to $\sqrt 2$. Starting with an initial guess, say $x_0=2$, we obtain the sequence
	\[
		x_1=\frac 12\left(2+\frac 22\right)=\frac 32,\quad x_2=\frac{17}{12} =1.4166\ldots,\quad x_3=\frac{577}{408}=1.4142\ldots,\quad \ldots
	\]
	This sequence certainly \emph{appears} to be converging to $\sqrt 2$\ldots
	\medbreak

	A rigorous proof that the sequence converges to $\sqrt 2$ requires more detail than is appropriate for this discussion (see Exercise \ref{exs:newtonconverge}). Two observations should make it seem more believable:
	\begin{enumerate}
	  \item \emph{If} the sequence defined by $(\ast)$ has a limit $L$, then it must satisfy
	  \[
	  	L=\frac 12\left(L+\frac 2L\right) \implies 2L^2=L^2+2\implies L^2=2\implies L=\sqrt 2
	  \]
	  where we take the positive root since all terms $x_n$ are plainly positive.
	  \item The algorithm can be interpreted \emph{geometrically}.\par
	  \begin{minipage}[t]{0.65\linewidth}\vspace{-14pt}
	  	The \textcolor{mGreen}{tangent line} to the \textcolor{Blue}{curve} $y=f(x)=x^2-2$ at $\big(x_n,f(x_n)\big)$ has equation
	  	\begin{align*}
	  		y&=f(x_n)+f'(x_n)(x-x_n)\\
	  		&=x_n^2-2+2x_n(x-x_n)\\
	  		&=2x_nx-x_n^2-2
	  	\end{align*}
	  	Its intersection with the $x$-axis yields the next iterate:
	  	\[
	  		y=0\implies x=\frac{x_n^2+2}{2x_n}=\frac 12\left(x_n+\frac 2{x_n}\right) =x_{n+1}
	  	\] 
	  \end{minipage}
	  \hfill
	  \begin{minipage}[t]{0.33\linewidth}\vspace{-18pt}
	  	\hfill\includegraphics[alt={the method of the mean approximating the square root of two}]{newton-raphson}
	  \end{minipage}
	\end{enumerate}
\end{example}


Since it makes use of the average, this approach is sometimes called the \emph{method of the mean}. It may be applied to approximate any square root $\sqrt a$ where $a>0$ (Exercise \ref{exs:methodmeangen}). Variations of this approach have been in use for thousands of years.
% It may be applied to any square-root $\sqrt a$ where $a>0$: let $x_0>0$ and define,
% \[
% 	x_{n+1}:=\frac 12\left(x_n+\frac a{x_n}\right) \tag{$\ast$}
% \]
\medbreak

More importantly, this geometric interpretation generalizes\ldots

\begin{defn}{}{}
	Given a differentiable function $f(x)$ with non-zero derivative, the \emph{Newton--Raphson iterates} of an initial value $x_0$ are defined by the recurrence formula
	\[
		x_{n+1}:=x_n-\frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}
	\]
\end{defn}

\begin{minipage}[t]{0.65\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
	Our two previous observations still hold:
	\begin{enumerate}
	  \item If $L=\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}x_n$ exists and $f'(L)\neq 0$, then
	  \[
	  	L=L-\frac{f(L)}{f'(L)}\implies f(L)=0
	  \]
		That is, the limit $L$ is a root of the \textcolor{Blue}{function $f(x)$}.
		\item The \textcolor{mGreen}{tangent line} at $\big(x_n,f(x_n)\big)$ forms a \textcolor{mGreen}{right-triangle} with base $x_n-x_{n+1}$ and height $f(x_n)$. Its slope is
		\[
			f'(x_n)=\frac{f(x_n)}{x_n-x_{n+1}}
		\]
		which rearranges to give the formula $x_{n+1}=x_n-\frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$.
	\end{enumerate}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.33\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
	\hfill\includegraphics[alt={newton's iterates using the tangent line}]{newton-raphson3}
\end{minipage}\smallbreak

For polynomials with integer coefficients, the iterates form a sequence of \emph{rational numbers}.


\begin{examples}{}{newtonex}
	We use Newton's method to find approximations to two irrational numbers.
	\begin{enumerate}
	  \item\label{ex:newtonex1} To find a root of $f(x)=x^4+4x-6$, start with $x_0=2$ and iterate\par
		\begin{minipage}[t]{0.65\linewidth}\vspace{-12pt}
			\[
				x_{n+1}=x_n-\frac{x_n^4+4x_n-6}{4x_n^3+4} =\frac{3(x_n^4+2)}{4(x_n^3+1)}
			\]
			which yields the sequence (to 3 d.p.)
			\[
				\left(2,\frac 32,\frac{339}{280},\ldots\right)=(2,1.5,1.211,1.121,1.114,1.114,\ldots)
			\]
			A calculator shows that 1.114 is approximately a root.
		\end{minipage}
		\hfill
		\begin{minipage}[t]{0.33\linewidth}\vspace{-12pt}
			\hfill\includegraphics[alt={newton's method for a quartic polynomial}]{newton-raphson2}
		\end{minipage}
	
		\item The irrational number $x=\sqrt 2+\sqrt 3$ is a root of the polynomial
		\[
			f(x)=x^4-10x^2+1
		\]
		Applying Newton's method with $x_0=3$ results in the sequence (to 3\,d.p.)
		\[
			x_{n+1}=x_n-\frac{x_n^4-10x_n^2+1}{4x_n^3-20x_n} \implies (x_n)=\left(3,\ \frac{19}6=3.167,\ %\frac{86569}{27512}=
			3.147,\ \ldots \right)
		\]
	\end{enumerate}
\end{examples}


Newton's method can be attempted for any differentiable function, though the sequence isn't guaranteed to converge: see for instance Exercise \ref{exs:newtonbad}. You can find graphical interfaces online for playing with this. Newton's method is one of the most important algorithms in numerical analysis, with many applications and generalizations to other contexts.

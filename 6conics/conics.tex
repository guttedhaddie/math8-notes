\section{Conic Sections}

We consider the traditional conic sections and relate their basic properties to modern equations.

\begin{defn}{}{conic}
	A \emph{(non-degenerate) conic section} is the intersection of a \emph{right circular cone}\footnotemark{} with a plane not containing the vertex. Depending on the orientation of the intersecting plane, we obtain one of the three standard types.
	\begin{description}
		\item[\emph{Parabola}] The plane intersects in a single curve either on the upper or lower half of the cone. Equivalently, the plane is parallel to one of the lines defining the cone.
		\item[\emph{Ellipse}] The intersection is a single closed curve.
		\item[\emph{Hyperbola}] The intersection comprises two curves, one each on the upper and lower halves of the cone. 
	\end{description}
\end{defn}

	\begin{tabular}{c@{\qquad\qquad}c@{\qquad}c}
		\includegraphics[alt={a parabola is where the plane is parallel to a generating line of the cone}]{conicparabola}
		&
		\includegraphics[alt={an ellipse is where the plane cuts the cone in a closed curve}]{conicellipse}
		&
		\includegraphics[alt={a hyperbola is where the plane cuts the cone in a pair of curves}]{conichyperbola}
		\\
		\phantom{b}\href{http://www.math.uci.edu/~ndonalds/math8/conicparabola2.html}{Parabola}

	A \emph{cone} is a surface obtained by joining all points on a curve with a fixed point, the \emph{vertex.} A right circular cone is when this curve may be chosen to be a \emph{circle} such that the line from its center to the vertex (the \emph{axis}) is perpendicular (\emph{right angles}) to the circle.%
}


\subsection{The Focus \& Directrix of a Parabola}

The above conic section definitions are hard to work with because they are three dimensional. We instead make alternative definitions as curves in the plane.

\begin{defn}{}{parabola}
	Let $d$ be a line and $F$ a point not on that line.\par
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.64\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
		The \emph{\textcolor{Blue}{parabola}} (in the plane spanned by $d$ and $P$) with \emph{directrix} $d$ and \emph{focus} $F$ is the set of points $P$ equidistant from both. As pictured, the parabola is the set of points for which
		\[
			\mathcolor{dGreen}{\nm{FP}}=\mathcolor{dGreen}{\nm{PQ}}
		\]
	\end{minipage}
	\hfill
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.35\linewidth}\vspace{-4\parsep}
		\hfill
		\includegraphics[alt={a parabola is the set of points equidistant from a given point and a line}]{parabola1}
	\end{minipage} 
\end{defn}

The definition is strictly \emph{synthetic} since equidistant really means that the line segments $\cl{FP}$ and $\cl{PQ}$ are \emph{congruent.} To interpret this \emph{analytically} we choose axes and co-ordinates.
\smallbreak

\begin{minipage}[t]{0.6\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
	Without loss of generality, choose the origin to lie on the parabola halfway between the focus and directrix, with the focus on the positive $y$-axis. Otherwise said, for some $a>0$, we have $F=(0,a)$ and the directrix is $y=-a$.
	\smallbreak
	Now we follow the definition: if $P=(x,y)$ is a point on the parabola, then $Q=(x,-a)$, whence
  \begin{align*}
	  \nm{FP}=\nm{PQ}
	  &\iff \nm{FP}^2=\nm{PQ}^2\\
	  &\iff x^2+(y-a)^2=(y+a)^2\\
	  &\iff x^2+y^2-2ay+a^2=y^2+2ay+a^2
  \end{align*}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.39\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
	\hfill\includegraphics[alt={a parabola using co-ordinates}]{parabola4}
\end{minipage}


This simplifies to recover a familiar equation:

\begin{thm}{Canonical form of a parabola}{parabolacanon}
	Given a parabola, there exist rectangular co-ordinates/axes with respect to which the parabola has equation
	\[
		x^2=4ay
	\]
	where $a>0$.
\end{thm}


Of course, parabolas are often not always presented in canonical co-ordinates.

\begin{example}{}{parabola}
	Find the focus and directrix of the parabola with equation $y=x^2-6x+7$.\smallbreak
	Start by completing the square!
	\[
		y=x^2-6x+7=(x-3)^2-2
	\]
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.65\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
		By defining new co-ordinates $X=x-3$ and $Y=y+2$, we obtain the canonical form
		\[
			X^2=Y=4aY
			\quad\text{where}\quad 
			a=\frac 14
		\]
		The focus has co-ordinates $(X,Y)=(0,\frac 14)$ and the directrix is $Y=-\frac 14$. Translating back to our original co-ordinates $(x,y)$, we see that the focus and directrix are
		\[
			F=\left(0+3,\frac 14-2\right)=\left(3,-\frac 74\right),\qquad d:y=-\frac 14-2=-\frac 94
		\]
	\end{minipage}
	\hfill
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.34\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
		\hfill\includegraphics[alt={a parabola in non-canonical co-ordinates}]{parabola3}
	\end{minipage}
\end{example}

Canonical co-ordinates can also be rotated relative to given axes, but this would take us too far afield. You will likely return to such ideas in an upper-division linear algebra course.


\subsubsection*{Why call it the \emph{Focus}?}

Imagine a \emph{parabolic mirror}: a reflective surface obtained by rotating a parabola around its canonical $y$-axis. The rays from a light source at `vertical infinity' (e.g., the sun) would arrive parallel to the $y$-axis. The parabolic mirror will \emph{focus} these rays onto the focus of the original parabola! This is the reason satellite dishes are parabolic, with the receiver placed at the focus.
\smallbreak

Convincing ourselves of this requires only a little triangle geometry. Let $P$ lie on the parabola, $Q$ be its nearest point on the directrix, and bisect angle $\angle FPQ$ with the line $\ell$. Let $R\neq P$ be any other point on $\ell$ and drop the perpendicular to $d$ at $S$.\par
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.6\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
	\begin{enumerate}
	  \item Side-Angle-Side for $\triangle FPR\cong\triangle QPR$ tells us that $\mathcolor{Brown}{\nm{FR}}=\mathcolor{Brown}{\nm{RQ}}$.
	  \item Since $\cl{RQ}$ is the hypotenuse of a right-triangle, we see that $\nm{RS}<\nm{RQ}=\nm{FR}$.
	  \item It follows that all points $R\neq P$ on $\ell$ \emph{lie on the same side of the parabola} (closer to $d$ than $F$), whence $\ell$ is \emph{tangent} to the parabola.
	  \item A vertical ray hitting the parabola at $P$ is reflected so that its angle of incidence equals its angle of reflection. Since $\ell$ is tangent, this angle equals $\mathcolor{mMagenta}{\angle QPR}\cong\mathcolor{mMagenta}{\angle FPR}$. The ray therefore reflected onto $\cl{PF}$ and passes through $F$.
	\end{enumerate}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.39\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
	\hfill\includegraphics[alt={a parabola reflecting parallel rays onto its focus}]{parabola2}
\end{minipage}

\subsection{Eccentricity: Ellipses and Hyperbolæ}

The words \emph{hyperbolic} and \emph{elliptical} have connotations of ``more than'' and ``less than.'' But \emph{more} or \emph{less} than what?\footnote{%
	The prefix \emph{para} means \emph{next to}, or \emph{akin to}: hence \emph{paranormal} or \emph{paralegal}. To speak \emph{hyperbolically} is to say more than you mean, to exaggerate. To be \emph{elliptical} is to be \emph{brief} or \emph{condensed}, possibly evasive.%
}
We simply modify Definition \ref{defn:parabola}.

\begin{defn}{Conics via Eccentricity}{}
	Let $d$ be a line and $F$ a point not on that line. Let $e>0$ be a constant. The \emph{conic} with focus $F$, \emph{directrix} $d$ and \emph{eccentricity} $e$ is the set of points $P$ for which
	\[
		\mathcolor{dGreen}{\nm{FP}}=e\mathcolor{dMagenta}{\nm{PQ}}
	\]
	where $Q\in d$ makes a perpendicular $\cl{PQ}\perp d$. The value of $e$ determines the type of conic:
	\begin{itemize}
	  \item $e<1$ yields an \textbf{\emph{ellipse}}; $P$ is \emph{closer} to the focus than the directrix.
	  \item $e=1$ a \textbf{\emph{parabola}};
	  \item $e>1$ a \textbf{\emph{hyperbola}}; $P$ is \emph{farther} from the focus than the directrix.
	\end{itemize}
\end{defn}

\begin{minipage}[t]{0.6\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
	To better understand these, draw the perpendicular $\lin{FD}$ to $d$ through $F$ and ask how many points of the curve lie on this line. Plainly there is a point $X_1$ \emph{between} $F$ and $d$, breaking the segment in the ratio $e:1$. Label distances $q,et,t$ as shown, where $q$ is the distance from $F$ to some other point $X_2$ on the curve. If $X_2$ exists, then
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.39\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
	\hfill\includegraphics[alt={finding a second point on the axis of symmetry}]{conic-def}
\end{minipage}

\[
	\nm{X_2F}=e\nm{X_2D}\iff q=e(q+et+t)\iff q(1-e)=e(1+e)t
\]
A unique second point $X_2$ therefore exists if and only if $e\neq 1$. To standardize things, choose axes so that $X_1,X_2$ lie on the $x$-axis at $x=\pm a$ ($a>0$). Then
\[
	q+et=2a\implies \left(\frac{e(1+e)}{1-e}+e\right)t=2a\implies t=\frac{1-e}ea=\left(\frac 1e-1\right)a,\quad q=(1+e)a
\]
The upshot is that we obtain two co-ordinate descriptions, where $F=(ae,0)$ and $d$ is the line $x=\frac ae$. We draw $F$ to the right of $d$ for the hyperbola since $t<0\Leftrightarrow e>1$.

	\begin{tabular}{c@{\qquad\qquad}c}
		\includegraphics[alt={the second point on the axis lies to the left on an ellipse}]{ellipse-def}
		&
		\includegraphics[alt={the second point on the axis lies to the right on a hyperbola}]{hyperbola-def}
		\\
		Ellipse: $e<1$
		&
		Hyperbola: $e>1$
	\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\pagebreak[3]

We need now only re-run the algebra from before Theorem \ref{thm:parabolacanon}: supposing $e\neq 1$,
  \begin{align*}
	  \nm{FP}=e\nm{PQ}
	  &\iff \nm{FP}^2=e^2\nm{PQ}^2\\
	  &\iff (x-ae)^2+y^2=e^2\left(\frac ae-x\right)^2\\
	  &\iff (1-e^2)x^2+y^2=a^2(1-e^2)\\
	  &\iff x^2+\frac{y^2}{a^2(1-e^2)}=1
  \end{align*}
  Not only is this familiar, it is \emph{symmetric} in $x$\ldots

\begin{thm}{Canonical form of Ellipse and Hyperbola}{}
	\phantom{bob}\par
	Given an ellipse, there exist rectangular co-ordinates/axes with respect to which the ellipse has equation
	\[
		\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1,\qquad 0<b\le a,\quad e=\sqrt{1-\frac{b^2}{a^2}}<1
	\]
	For a hyperbola, the corresponding equation is
	\[
		\frac{x^2}{a^2}-\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1,\qquad e=\sqrt{1+\frac{b^2}{a^2}}>1 \tag{$b$ can be larger than $a$ here}
	\]
	Both conics have \emph{two} foci $F,G=(\pm ae,0)$ and \emph{two} directrices $x=\pm\frac ae$.
\end{thm}

Both directrices/foci in double picture

While a parabola has only one focus, typical ellipses and hyperbolæ have \emph{two}. 

\begin{defn}{Ellipse and Hyperbola using Foci}{}
	Let $F,G$ be points in the plane.
	\begin{enumerate}
	  \item The \emph{ellipse} with \emph{foci} $F,G$ and \emph{semi-major axis} $a>\frac 12\nm{FG}$ is the set of points $P$ satisfying
		\[
			\mathcolor{dGreen}{\nm{FP}}+\mathcolor{dMagenta}{\nm{GP}}=2a
		\]
		\begin{center}\vspace{-\belowdisplayskip}\vspace{-2\parsep}
			\begin{tabular}{c@{\qquad\qquad}c}
				\includegraphics[alt={an ellipse is the locus of points for which the sum of the distances to two fixed points is constant}]{ellipse1}
				&
				\includegraphics[alt={a hyperbola is the locus of points for which the difference of the distances to two fixed points is constant}]{hyperbola1}
				\\
				An ellipse with foci $F,G$
				&
				A hyperbola with foci $F,G$
			\end{tabular}\vspace{-\parsep}
		\end{center}
		\item The \emph{hyperbola} with \emph{foci} $F,G$ and \emph{semi-major axis} $a<\frac 12\nm{FG}$ is the set of points $P$ such that
		\[
			\mathcolor{dGreen}{\nm{FP}}-\mathcolor{dMagenta}{\nm{GP}}=\pm 2a
		\]
	\end{enumerate}\vspace{-\belowdisplayskip}
\end{defn}

The interior and boundary of an ellipse are the locations you stand while holding a rope of fixed length whose ends are attached to the foci $F,G$. If $F=G$, the ellipse is a circle with radius $a$.\smallbreak

Choosing co-ordinates so that $F=(c,0)$ and $G=(-c,0)$ lie on the $x$-axis, we see that the points $P=(x,y)$ on the ellipse have co-ordinates satisfying\par
\begin{minipage}[b]{0.65\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
\begin{gather*}
	\begin{aligned}
		(x-c)^2+y^2&=\nm{FP}^2
			=\big(2a-\nm{GP}\big)^2
			=\left(2a-\sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2}\right)^2\hspace{-50pt}\\
		&=4a^2+(x+c)^2+y^2-4a\sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2}
	\end{aligned}\\
	\implies a\sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2}=a^2+cx\\
	\implies a^2\left(x^2+2cx+c^2+y^2\right)=a^4+2a^2cx+c^2x^2\\
	\implies (a^2-c^2)x^2+a^2y^2=a^2(a^2-c^2)
\end{gather*}
Divide through by $a^2(a^2-c^2)$ to obtain\ldots
\end{minipage}

\subsubsection*{Eccentricity}

The obvious difference between our descriptions of the ellipse/hyperbola versus the parabola is the \emph{directrix.}

\begin{thm}{}{}
Let $F$ be a point (the \emph{focus}) not on a line $d$ (the \emph{directrix}) and choose a number $e>0$ (the \emph{eccentricity}). Consider the set of points $P$ satisfying
\[\nm{PF}=e\nm{PQ}\]
where $Q$ is the nearest point to $P$ on the directrix.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Depending on the value of $e$ we obtain one of the three conic sections. For the ellipse and the hyperbola this construction only produces half of the conic; there are in fact \emph{two} directrices.
	\begin{center}
		\begin{tabular}{c@{\qquad}c@{\qquad\qquad}c}
		\includegraphics{eccentricity2}
		&
		\includegraphics{eccentricity}
		&
		\includegraphics{eccentricity3}
		\\
		Ellipse $e<1$&Parabola $e=1$&Hyperbola $e>1$
		\end{tabular}
	\end{center}
	\item For the ellipse and hyperbola $e=\frac ca$, where $c=\frac 12\nm{FG}$.
	%\item If a plane intersects a right circular cone $z^2=x^2+y^2$ at an angle of $\alpha$ to the horizontal (i.e. $z=\tan\alpha y+k$), then $e=\sqrt 2\sin\alpha$ (max $e=\sqrt 2$, whence plane vertical)
\end{enumerate}
\end{thm}

By convention, a circle is considered to have eccentricity $e=0$, even though the above doesn't apply. We could imagine obtaining a circle of radius $a$ by taking the limit $e\to 0^+$ (i.e.\ $c\to 0^+$). In such a limit, the point $Q$ on the directrix must go to infinity: circles therefore do not have directrices!

\begin{example}{}{}
The canonical form ellipse $\frac{x^2}{25}+\frac{y^2}{16}=1$ has $a=5,b=4$ and $c=\sqrt{a^2-b^2}=3$. Its eccentricity is therefore $e=\frac ca=\frac 35$. Since its foci have co-ordinates $(\pm\frac 35,0)$, its right directrix must be a vertical line which intersects the $x$-axis when
\[e(x-a)=(a-c)\implies x=a+\frac{a-c}e=5+\frac{10}3=\frac{25}3\]
\end{example}

\subsubsection*{Conics in Polar Co-ordinates}

\begin{minipage}[t]{0.7\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
A fun application is to recover polar forms for the standard conics. We use the focus/directrix/eccentricity definition, place the origin at a focus $F$ and directrix $d$ vertically to the right of the origin. Recall that a conic with eccentricity $e$ may be described as the set of points $P$ such that $\nm{PF}=e\nm{Pd}$. Exactly one point on the conic ($K$) lies between the focus and directrix. If we let $\nm{KF}=k$, it should be clear from the picture that
\[\frac re+r\cos\theta=k+\frac ke \implies r=\frac{k(1+e)}{1+e\cos\theta}\]
This is \emph{independent} of the type of conic, and even works when $e=0$; $r=2k$ is a circle with radius $k$!
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.29\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
\hfill\includegraphics{eccentric}
\end{minipage}

\begin{itemize}
  \item If $e<0$, then $1+e\cos\theta>0$ for all $\theta$ and $r$ is always defined. We obtain a closed curve: an ellipse.
  \item If $e=1$, then $1+\cos\theta=0$ when $\theta=\pi$. We obtain a parabola opening leftwards.
  \item If $e>1$, then $1+e\cos\theta=0$ at two values $\theta=\pm\cos^{-1}\frac 1e$. These lines are parallel to the asymptotes of the hyperbola. Permitting $r<0$, the remaining values of $\theta$ give the other branch of the hyperbola!
\end{itemize}

    \[\diff[^2]{t^2}(r\hvr) =-\frac b{r^2}\hvr\]
    for some positive constant $b$. This is Newton's model of gravity.\par
    Check that \emph{every conic} is a solution to this differential equation.\par
    (\emph{Hint: use the fact that $r^2\dot\theta$ is constant! This is essentially conservation of angular momentum}) 
  \end{enumerate}
  This explains Kepler's 1\st\ law, that planets orbit the sun in ellipses with the sun at one focus. The other conical orbits are indeed possible: comets regularly pass the sun along hyperbolic orbits, never to return to the solar system.
\end{enumerate}

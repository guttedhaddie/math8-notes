\section{Conic Sections}

We consider the traditional conic sections and relate their basic properties to modern equations.

\begin{defn}{}{conic}
	A \emph{(non-degenerate) conic section} is the intersection of a \emph{right circular cone}\footnotemark{} with a plane not containing the vertex. Depending on the orientation of the intersecting plane, we obtain one of the three standard types.
	\begin{description}
		\item[\emph{Parabola}] The plane intersects in a single curve either on the upper or lower half of the cone. Equivalently, the plane is parallel to one of the lines defining the cone.
		\item[\emph{Ellipse}] The intersection is a single closed curve.
		\item[\emph{Hyperbola}] The intersection comprises two curves, one each on the upper and lower halves of the cone. 
	\end{description}
\end{defn}

	\begin{tabular}{c@{\qquad\qquad}c@{\qquad}c}
		\includegraphics[alt={a parabola is where the plane is parallel to a generating line of the cone}]{conicparabola}
		&
		\includegraphics[alt={an ellipse is where the plane cuts the cone in a closed curve}]{conicellipse}
		&
		\includegraphics[alt={a hyperbola is where the plane cuts the cone in a pair of curves}]{conichyperbola}
		\\
		\phantom{b}\href{http://www.math.uci.edu/~ndonalds/math8/conicparabola2.html}{Parabola}

\footnotetext{%
	A \emph{cone} is a surface obtained by joining all points on a curve with a fixed point, the \emph{vertex.} A right circular cone is when this curve may be chosen to be a \emph{circle} such that the line from its center to the vertex (the \emph{axis}) is perpendicular (\emph{right angles}) to the circle.%
}


\subsection{The Focus \& Directrix of a Parabola}

The above conic section definitions are hard to work with because they are three dimensional. We instead make alternative definitions as curves in the plane.

\begin{defn}{}{parabola}
	Let $d$ be a line and $F$ a point not on that line.\par
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.64\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
		The \emph{\textcolor{Blue}{parabola}} (in the plane spanned by $d$ and $P$) with \emph{directrix} $d$ and \emph{focus} $F$ is the set of points $P$ equidistant from both. As pictured, the parabola is the set of points for which
		\[
			\mathcolor{dGreen}{\nm{FP}}=\mathcolor{dGreen}{\nm{PQ}}
		\]
	\end{minipage}
	\hfill
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.35\linewidth}\vspace{-4\parsep}
		\hfill
		\includegraphics[alt={a parabola is the set of points equidistant from a given point and a line}]{parabola1}
	\end{minipage} 
\end{defn}

The definition is strictly \emph{synthetic} since equidistant really means that the line segments $\cl{FP}$ and $\cl{PQ}$ are \emph{congruent.} To interpret this \emph{analytically} we choose axes and co-ordinates.
\smallbreak

\begin{minipage}[t]{0.6\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
	Without loss of generality, choose the origin to lie on the parabola halfway between the focus and directrix, with the focus on the positive $y$-axis. Otherwise said, for some $a>0$, we have $F=(0,a)$ and the directrix is $y=-a$.
	\smallbreak
	Now we follow the definition: if $P=(x,y)$ is a point on the parabola, then $Q=(x,-a)$, whence
  \begin{align*}
	  \nm{FP}=\nm{PQ}
	  &\iff \nm{FP}^2=\nm{PQ}^2\\
	  &\iff x^2+(y-a)^2=(y+a)^2\\
	  &\iff x^2+y^2-2ay+a^2=y^2+2ay+a^2
  \end{align*}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.39\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
	\hfill\includegraphics[alt={a parabola using co-ordinates}]{parabola4}
\end{minipage}


This simplifies to recover a familiar equation:

\begin{thm}{Canonical form of a parabola}{parabolacanon}
	Given a parabola, there exist rectangular co-ordinates/axes with respect to which the parabola has equation
	\[
		x^2=4ay
	\]
	where $a>0$.
\end{thm}


Of course, parabolas are often not always presented in canonical co-ordinates.

\begin{example}{}{parabola}
	Find the focus and directrix of the parabola with equation $y=x^2-6x+7$.\smallbreak
	Start by completing the square!
	\[
		y=x^2-6x+7=(x-3)^2-2
	\]
	By defining new co-ordinates $X=x-3$ and $Y=y+2$, we obtain the canonical form
	\[
		X^2=Y=4aY
		\quad\text{where}\quad 
		a=\frac 14
	\]
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.65\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
		The focus has co-ordinates $(X,Y)=(0,\frac 14)$ and the directrix is $\smash{Y=-\frac 14}$. Translating back to our original co-ordinates $(x,y)$, we see that the focus and directrix are
		\begin{gather*}
			F=\left(0+3,\frac 14-2\right)=\left(3,-\frac 74\right)\\
			d:y=-\frac 14-2=-\frac 94
		\end{gather*}
	\end{minipage}
	\hfill
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.34\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
		\hfill\includegraphics[alt={a parabola in non-canonical co-ordinates}]{parabola3}
	\end{minipage}
\end{example}

Canonical co-ordinates can also be rotated relative to given axes, but this would take us too far afield. You will likely return to such ideas in an upper-division linear algebra course.


\subsubsection*{Why call it the \emph{Focus}?}\label{pg:parabolafocus}

Imagine a \emph{parabolic mirror}: a reflective surface obtained by rotating a parabola around its canonical $y$-axis. The rays from a light source at `vertical infinity' (e.g., the sun) would arrive parallel to the $y$-axis. The parabolic mirror will \emph{focus} these rays onto the focus of the original parabola! This is the reason satellite dishes are parabolic with the receiver placed at the focus.
\smallbreak

Convincing ourselves of this requires only a little triangle geometry. Let $P$ lie on the parabola, $Q$ be the nearest point on the directrix, and bisect angle $\angle FPQ$ with the line $\ell$. Let $R\neq P$ be any other point on $\ell$ and drop the perpendicular to $d$ at $S$.\par
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.61\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
	\begin{enumerate}
	  \item Side-Angle-Side for $\triangle FPR\cong\triangle QPR$ tells us that $\mathcolor{Brown}{\nm{FR}}=\mathcolor{Brown}{\nm{RQ}}$.
	  \item Since $\cl{RQ}$ is the hypotenuse of a right-triangle, we see that $\nm{RS}<\nm{RQ}=\nm{FR}$.
	  \item All points $R\neq P$ on $\ell$ therefore \emph{lie on the same side of the parabola} (closer to $d$ than $F$), whence $\ell$ is \emph{tangent} to the parabola.
	  \item A vertical ray hitting the parabola at $P$ is reflected so that its angle of incidence equals its angle of reflection. Since $\ell$ is tangent, this angle is $\mathcolor{mMagenta}{\angle QPR}\cong\mathcolor{mMagenta}{\angle FPR}$. The ray is therefore reflected to $\cl{PF}$ and passes through $F$.
	\end{enumerate}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.37\linewidth}\vspace{0pt}
	\hfill\includegraphics[alt={a parabola reflecting parallel rays onto its focus}]{parabola2}
\end{minipage}

\subsection{Eccentricity: Ellipses and Hyperbolæ}

The words \emph{hyperbola} and \emph{ellipse} have connotations of ``more than'' and ``less than.'' But \emph{more} or \emph{less} than what?\footnote{%
	The prefix \emph{para} means \emph{next to}, or \emph{akin to}, alike but not equal: e.g., \emph{parallel}, \emph{paranormal} or \emph{paralegal}. To speak \emph{hyperbolically} is to say more than you mean, to exaggerate. To be \emph{elliptical} is to be brief or condensed, possibly evasive; \emph{ellipsis} are dots (\ldots) indicating something omitted.%
}
The answer lies in a simple modification of Definition \ref{defn:parabola}.

\begin{defn}{Conics via Eccentricity}{}
	Let $d$ be a line and $F$ a point not on that line. Let $e>0$ be a constant. The \emph{conic} with focus $F$, \emph{directrix} $d$ and \emph{eccentricity} $e$ is the set of points $P$ for which
	\[
		\mathcolor{dGreen}{\nm{FP}}=e\mathcolor{dMagenta}{\nm{PQ}}
	\]
	where $Q\in d$ makes a perpendicular $\cl{PQ}\perp d$. The value of $e$ determines the type of conic:
	\begin{itemize}
	  \item $e<1$ yields an \textbf{\emph{ellipse}}; $P$ is \emph{closer} to the focus than the directrix ($\mathcolor{dGreen}{\nm{FP}}$ is smaller than $\mathcolor{dMagenta}{\nm{PQ}}$).
	  \item $e=1$ a \textbf{\emph{parabola}};
	  \item $e>1$ a \textbf{\emph{hyperbola}}; $P$ is \emph{farther} from the focus than the directrix.
	\end{itemize}
\end{defn}


To better understand these, it is helpful to draw axes. We do this explicitly for the ellipse:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Let the $x$-axis be the perpendicular $\lin{FD}$ to $d$ through $F$, where $D$ is to the right of $F$.
	\item There exists a point $X$ on the curve \emph{between} $F$ and $d$, breaking the segment in the ratio $e:1$.
	\item Define $a=\frac{\nm{FX}}{1-e}$ and slide the axis left or right until $X=(a,0)$.
	\item Then $F=\big(a-\nm{FX},0\big)=\big(a-a(1-e),0\big)=(ae,0)$ and the directrix is $x=a+\frac 1e\nm{FX}=\frac ae$.
\end{enumerate} 

The picture is drawn below. In the limit $e\to 0^+$, the focus becomes $F=(0,0)$, the point $Q$ moves to $\infty$, and the ellipse becomes a \emph{circle} of radius $a$ (circles do not have a directrix).\smallbreak
For the hyperbola, convention places $D$ to the \emph{left} of $F$ (step 1) and defines $a=\frac{\nm{FX}}{e-1}$ (step 3). This results in identical expressions for the focus and directrix.
\begin{center}
	\begin{tabular}{c@{\qquad\qquad}c}
		\includegraphics[alt={the second point on the axis lies to the left on an ellipse}]{ellipse-def}
		&
		\includegraphics[alt={the second point on the axis lies to the right on a hyperbola}]{hyperbola-def}
		\\
		Ellipse: $e<1$
		&
		Hyperbola: $e>1$
	\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\pagebreak[3]

We need now only re-run the algebra prior to Theorem \ref{thm:parabolacanon} to obtain the familiar co-ordinate form: supposing $e\neq 1$,
\begin{align*}
	\nm{FP}=e\nm{PQ}
	&\iff \nm{FP}^2=e^2\nm{PQ}^2\\
	&\iff (x-ae)^2+y^2=e^2\left(\frac ae-x\right)^2\\
	&\iff (1-e^2)x^2+y^2=a^2(1-e^2)\\
	&\iff x^2+\frac{y^2}{a^2(1-e^2)}=1
\end{align*}


\begin{thm}{Canonical form of Ellipse and Hyperbola}{}
	\phantom{bob}\par
	Given an ellipse with eccentricity $e$, there exist rectangular co-ordinates/axes with respect to which the ellipse has equation
	\[
		\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1,\qquad 0<b\le a,\quad e=\sqrt{1-\frac{b^2}{a^2}}<1
	\]
	For a hyperbola, the corresponding equation is
	\[
		\frac{x^2}{a^2}-\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1,\qquad 0<a,b,\quad e=\sqrt{1+\frac{b^2}{a^2}}>1 \tag{$b$ can be larger than $a$ for a hyperbola}
	\]
	By symmetry in $x$, both conics have two foci $F,G=(\pm ae,0)$ and two directrices $x=\pm\frac ae$. The conics may be defined using either pair.\smallbreak
	The values $a,b$ are, respectively, the \textbf{semi-major} and \textbf{semi-minor} axes. 
\end{thm}

\begin{center}
	\begin{tabular}{c@{\qquad}c}
		\includegraphics[alt={a canonical ellipse with two foci and two directrices}]{ellipse-def2}
		&
		\includegraphics[alt={a canonical hyperbola with two foci and two directrices}]{hyperbola-def2}
		\\
		Ellipse $e<1$
		&
		Hyperbola $e>1$
	\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The canonical forms make clear why there are precisely two points of an ellipse or hyperbola on the $x$-axis: $x=\pm a$. For a hyperbola, $b$ is mostly irrelevant except inasmuch as the curve is seen to approach its \emph{\textcolor{Brown}{asymptotes}} $y=\pm\frac ba$.

\pagebreak[3]


\begin{examples}[breakable=false]{}{}
	In general, to put a conic in canonical form we must complete the square \emph{twice}.
	\begin{enumerate}
	  \item The canonical form hyperbola
	  \[
	  	\frac{(x+1)^2}{9}-\frac{(y-7)^2}{16}=1
	  \]
	  has canonical co-ordinates $X=x+1$, $Y=y-y$ and parameters
	  \[
	  	a=3,\quad b=4,\quad e=\sqrt{1+\frac{b^2}{a^2}}=\frac 53
	  \]
	  Its foci and directrices are
	  \begin{gather*}
	  	F,G=(-1,7)\pm(ae,0)=(4,7),(-6,7)\\
			x=-1\pm\frac ae =-1\pm\frac 95=-\frac{14}5,\frac 45
	  \end{gather*}
	
		\item Given the conic with equation
		\[
			9x^2+4y^2-36x-8y+4=0\tag{$\ast$}
		\]
		we complete the square.\par
		\begin{minipage}[t]{0.68\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}\vspace{-\abovedisplayskip}
			\begin{align*}
				(\ast)&\iff 9(x-2)^2+4(y-1)^2+4-36-4=0\\
				&\iff \frac{(y-1)^2}{9}+\frac{(x-2)^2}{4}=1
			\end{align*}
			Be careful with the denominators! This is an \emph{ellipse} with canonical co-ordinates $X=y-1$, $Y=x-2$ and parameters
			\[
				a=3>b=2,\quad e=\sqrt{1-\frac 49}=\frac{\sqrt 5}3
			\]
			The foci and directrices are
			\begin{gather*}
				F,G=(1,2\pm ae)=(1,2\pm\sqrt 5)\\
				y=1\pm\frac ae=1\pm\frac 9{\sqrt 5}
			\end{gather*}\vspace{-\parsep}
		\end{minipage}
		\hfill
		\begin{minipage}[t]{0.31\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
			\hfill\includegraphics[alt={an ellipse in non-canonical co-ordinates}]{ellipse-ex}
		\end{minipage}
	\end{enumerate}
\end{examples}

Rotated co-ordinates $(X,Y)$ can be used if there is an $xy$-term, though such is beyond this course. We are now in a position to describe any conic whose equation has the form
\[
	\lambda x^2+\kappa y^2+\sigma x+\tau y+\mu=0
\]
The type of conic depends on the signs of $\lambda$ and $\kappa$.

\subsubsection*{The Reflection Principle for an Ellipse}

An ellipse has \emph{two} foci: so what are they focusing? First we make a simple observation.

\begin{lemm}{Distance Sum from the Foci}{}
	\phantom{bob}\par
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.55\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
		Suppose an ellipse has foci $F,G$ and semi-major axis $a$.\par
		Then the points on the ellipse are precisely those satisfying
		\[
			\mathcolor{Brown}{\nm{FP}}+\mathcolor{dMagenta}{\nm{GP}}=e\nm{QQ'}=2a
		\]
	\end{minipage}
	\hfill
	\begin{minipage}[t]{0.44\linewidth}\vspace{-4.8\parsep}
		\hfill\includegraphics[alt={for an ellipse, the sum of the distances to the foci is constant}]{ellipse-def3}
	\end{minipage}	
\end{lemm}

Imagine holding onto a rope (length $2a$) whose ends are tied to two posts ($F,G$). The \textcolor{Blue}{ellipse} marks the locations where the rope would be fully taut.
\medbreak

We are now in a position to mimic the parabolic discussion on page \pageref{pg:parabolafocus}.\par
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.64\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
	\begin{itemize}
	  \item Given $P$ on an ellipse with foci $F,G$, extend $\cl{GP}$ to $Q$ such that $\mathcolor{Brown}{\nm{PQ}}=\mathcolor{Brown}{\nm{PF}}$, and bisect $\mathcolor{mGreen}{\angle FPQ}$ by a line $\ell$.
	  \item If $R$ is any other point on $\ell$, then
	  \begin{align*}
	  	\mathcolor{mGreen}{\nm{FR}}+\nm{GR}
	  	&=\mathcolor{mGreen}{\nm{QR}}+\nm{GR}\\
	  	&\overset{\triangle}{>}\nm{GQ}
	  	=\mathcolor{Brown}{\nm{PQ}}+\mathcolor{mMagenta}{\nm{GP}}\\
	  	&=\mathcolor{Brown}{\nm{FP}}+\mathcolor{mMagenta}{\nm{GP}}=2a
	  \end{align*}
	  where we used the \emph{triangle-inequality.}
	  \item All points $R\neq P$ on $\ell$ therefore lie \emph{outside} the ellipse, whence $\ell$ is \emph{tangent} to the ellipse at $P$.
	  \item If the ellipse is thought of as a mirror, a light source placed at one focus will have its rays reflected onto the other focus.
	\end{itemize}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.35\linewidth}\vspace{-\parsep}
	\hfill\includegraphics[alt={constructing a line tangent to the ellipse by angle bisection}]{ellipse3}\smallbreak
	\hfill\includegraphics[alt={light rays bouncing from one focus to another}]{ellipse4}
\end{minipage}
\medbreak


Equivalently, suppose you were playing mini-golf with the ball at one focus and the hole at the other. You could play your stroke \emph{in any direction} and, provided you hit it hard enough,\footnote{%
	And the ball bounced off the wall perfectly so that the angle of incidence equalled that of reflection; due to the complex nature of collisions, this typically \emph{does not happen} in real life.%
}
the ball would land in the hole!

	  The first points away from the origin; the second is obtained by rotating the first counter-clockwise \ang{90}.
	  \begin{enumerate}
	    \item Use the chain rule to verify that $\diff t\hvr=\dot\theta\hth$, where $\dot r=\diff[r]{t}$ means differentiation with respect to time. What is $\diff t\hth$ in terms of $\hvr$?
	    
	    \item Verify that
	    \[
	    	\diff[^2]{t^2}\big(r\hvr\big) =(\ddot r-r\dot\theta^2)\hvr+\frac 1r\left[\diff t\big(r^2\dot\theta\big)\right]\hth
	    \]
	    Note that $r\hvr$ is the position vector of the point $P$.
	     
	    \item Suppose an object travels along \emph{any conic} whose focus is at the origin in such a fashion that its \emph{speed} $r\dot\theta\propto \frac 1r$ is inversely proportional to its distance from the origin: equivalently $r^2\dot\theta=L$ is constant.\par
	    Use the polar form (Exercise \ref{exs:polarconic}) to show that the curve satisfies the differential equation
	    \[
	    	\diff[^2]{t^2}\big(r\hvr\big) =-\frac c{r^2}\hvr \tag{$\ast$}
	    \]
	    where $c$ is a positive constant. Compute $c$.
	  \end{enumerate}
	  This is Newton's model of gravity: following his second law ($\vF=m\va$), the object is subject to an \emph{inverse-square} attractive force. If you want a challenge, the converse is also true: ($\ast$) implies that the orbit is a conic whose focus is at the origin.\par
	  This discussion relates to Kepler's 1\st\ law, that planets orbit the sun in ellipses with the sun at one focus. The constancy of $r^2\dot\theta$ is essentially the conservation of angular momentum. The other types of orbit are also possible: while recurring comets (e.g., Halley's) follow elliptic orbits, some have been known to pass the sun along hyperbolic orbits, never to return to the solar system.
	\end{enumerate}
\end{exercises}
